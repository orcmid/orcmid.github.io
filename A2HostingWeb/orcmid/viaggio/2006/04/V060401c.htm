Renovated West Seattle Branch Library, May 2004
Dedication plaque for the original library on this site.

Built in 1910 with funds donated by Andrew Carnegie, the building of the West Seattle branch  public library was renovated in 2003-2004.  The renovation was funded by a voter-approved bond initiative that also supported the construction of the new central library.

In May 2004, I had just checked out a stack of mysteries for Vicki and I to read while she was convalescing.  That was my first geek-librarian experience.  They had installed new self-check-out stations that automatically identified the books and would check them out to my library card (so long as I knew my PIN number, which I did).  The librarian was telling me what to do rather than give me time to read the displays, understand what the choices were, and figure it out myself.  Now I will have to do it over again to actually learn it.  Next time, if someone offers to be helpful I will explain how that doesn't work for me. 

I suppose it's geek karma.   I am certain I have leaned over and worked someone else's keyboard and mouse so I could show them how to do something.  [dh:2005-11-17 The librarians are now more confident of the patrons, and vice versa, and the self-checkout works without hiccups, generally.]

This is a small library with high ceilings and open spaces.  Although there are computer tables and modern lighting, everything has been done to preserve the original woodworking and maintain the style and feel of the original structure.  Even the adjustments for accessibility, bicycles, and parking are added to the site in ways that preserve the experience of the building.

I like the new, modern libraries, especially in the surrounding King County system.  At the same time, there is something wonderful about how this library has been preserved and kept in its neighborhood setting.
