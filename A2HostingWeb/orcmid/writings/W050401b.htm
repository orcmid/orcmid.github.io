Ron D. Lee

Setting the stage on what the debate is.  The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), National Security Letters/Section 215.

We at the FBI do not want your secrets unles you are a terrorist or spy."  -- How do we know if you are one?

The basic needs of governments is to get and undrestand the information nuggest needed to save lives, ....

Connection the dots - knowing what dots to look for, and getting it to people who can act on it

Table of sourcs of information and meaning.  E.g., walking to the coffee shop (plain view information).

Sources of Information and Meaning: Under Executive Orders (e.g., 12333), statutory, law enforcement, public health/safety, regulatory, private sector, plain view.

FISA Fundamentals - foreign powers and agents of same: US persons, non-US persons.

Surveillance orders issued by a special Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court.  AG must sign off, and a handfull of senior officials has to certify that there is a significant foreign intelligence purpose. 

FISC is made up of 3 sitting judges on staggered 7-year terms, and there is a review/appelate court and the Supremes are above that.

"The Wall."  Keeping criminal investigators from FISA activities, and they might stear the activity away from the primary purpose of FISA surveillance.

So FISA surveillance would not deliver information to non-intelligence investigators.  Lowering the wall allows more interaction.

National Security Letters: A written certification to obtain certain information for foreign counterintelligence purposes.  Does not require a court order.  Not related to FISA.

NSL gets financial institutions and credit reporting, telephony, financial records, travel, etc., and connection with other people who are suspect.

A court has held that NSLs violate ammendments 1 and 4.  This is under appeal.

Section 215 on business records is part of Patriot Act.  Also has a nondisclosure requirement.

There was an earlier counterpart, rquiring a linkage and the person to whom the records pertained were a foreign power or an agent of one.

Issue with non-disclosure, how to challenge it and get counsel. 

Organizational issues.

Teen-Age Panel

5 teens and Dana Boyd.

Peter: Average local teens, etc.  Only found exceptional and opinionated teens.

Introduction of Dana Boyd. An "Ethnographic Engineer" at Google.  Using ethnography in seeing how people employ technology in presentation of self.

Dana introduces the students -

Technologies - IM is huge with youth culture - E-mail is dead among the youth; IM dominates and that's AOL Instant Messenger

All of the panelists use it, and there friends use it.  For social reasons, just to chat or talk, sharing homework notes, etc.  Another form of telephone, more personal than e-mail, immediate responses, many people (10-20 windows open at once), etc. more private than telephone because not being overheard.  For their social life, it is very important.  More important than F2F, not so challenging with IM.  One kid has been prevented from using AIM by dad, who is completely anxious about it.  Also banned at school and a student finally found a service that is not blocked by the school.  All IM and blogs are blocked at one school, and as a school paper editor, it is very handicapping.  Discussion about blocking by schools.

Discussion about running with logs on or not.

Mobile phones.  Feel too much like a leash -- don't want the folks calling all the time.  Don't like others presuming that they can be reached all the time.  Other only uses the phone to make calls and keeps it off all the time. One uses it to stay connected.

Text messaging.  Many kids do it in class without being caught.  It is used during tests to transfer answers.  It works where you wouldn't be allowed to use a phone otherwise.  Also at night when don't want to disturb someone.

Location based services.  How would that work.  The anti-leash guy doesn't want it.  Wants to activate it only when he wants.   All want a switch on it. 

School systems with RFID tracking.   Kids would find all kinds of ways to pass the tag around, avoid surveillance. 

Blog rings and ranting, spewing, and then what about it being public??  Social stuff and the ways teens deal with disliking other people, etc.  Use them for emotional release.  Safety valve.  An impersonal safety valve.  Rateyourteacher.com. 

One of the fears teachers have is that blogs are used to talk about them.  A teacher can use it as a way to find out what the relationship is. They talk about them anyway, now they know it.  

Exposure to the ever-present sexual preditors.  Some kids are not as careful as they should be.  But known cases of it were off-Internet.  Kids need to be careful and use common sense.

What about hiding information that could be used to find you in the real world.  Not talking to people that they don't know and haven't met.  It is offered as a reason not to blog, but it doesn't stop the kids.  Talking to someone with referrals of other friends are mostly OK.  Lots of discussion about common sense.

Myspace profiles, not required to disclose identifying information, but interest information. You can fake your identity on  Myspaces, you can use false information and your friends know who it is.  Most peers put up real information.  Some kids disclose a great deal that could be used to locate or encounter them.

Talking about P2P and song-sharing, etc.  It is not so public now and no one is talking about it so much. With Bit Torrent you have to know what you want.  It is not so open any more, or burning copies of songs that other people have, very personal and not the anonymous broadcast stuff.  Now you get music from friends and they file swap or burn CDs.  IM is used to move pictures more than music.

Kids think it is illegal if there is no money involved.  (Was corrected.)  That is a common adult misconception.

Gmail is used that way too, because the attachment and storage sizes are so generous.

RIAA behavior had kids stop buying CDs. 

School filtering systems.  Kids say it doesn't really work.  It interferes but doesn't block porn. There is the problem about hurting kids not being able to see blogs, IM, etc.  All message boards are blocked by one company but not other sites.  The blocking is capricious.  If you want to get around it you can, but if you are just trying to do some work, and it gets in the way.  Seems arbitrary.  Also filter religious sites.  Lots of pointless interference.  Schools should not be making moral decisions for the kids.

"Harmful to minors" material.  Rather than prohibit, it should be taught.  If kids can't see it, why is it allowed at all?  Filtering is a problem because language is not partitioned like that.  Like language among students.

Question about first-amendment going too far.  Some understand the subtlety and difference between speech and action, and it seems that the kids don't understand what the first-amendment means.  Being allowed to be who we are.  Kids see the first amendment as broader than it actually is. 

Broader thinking about privacy.   Do kids care about that other than hiding from the folks. The concern about parental surveillance, and transference to government and businesses as kids grow.  Don't stop the kid use of technology, make it work properly.  Most kids don't have any fear about privacy since they haven't run into it.  Parents need to talk to their kids more.  There is a different pace that the kids move at, but adults aren't aware of what the kids are doing, even with the same vehicles.  Parents may not be using opportunities to get to know their kids. 

Managing relationships - different approaches to changing screen names, avoiding people, preventing incoming mail, etc.

Interesting problem when parents show distrust by their conduct.  Problems about trust breakdown would lead to not using the family machine or find other approaches.  Alternative to F2F, and is it being diminished by having digital comm?  So there is an extended maintenance of distant relationships.

How do you feel when someone is ranting about you?  Interesting about knowing that people can be ranting even if you don't know about it.  The teen peer stuff works.  They don't take it personally.  Some friendships have been renewed.  It is when it wouldn't come up F2F, it can be hurtful to a relationship. Lots of interpersonal noise, and the digital works no differently.

Kids get apathetic about 9/11, and the green-orange-red thing.

Youth culture uses Zanga and Live Journal and they operate as communities among the youth, and there is a way to limit a post to viewing by friends.  It is more social stuff, talking about their lives, not as publishing

My Space is an interation of Friendster used by youth.

Il-Horn Hann from USC on "Online Information Privacy."  Raises the cost-benefit trade-off.  Privacy is the right to be left alone.

Economics, Duke.  Privacy and Information Acquisition in Competitive Markets.  Great story of 14-year-old daughter found to have a fully public blog, and when confronted about it accused her parents of invading her privacy.  Price discrimination appropriate (insurance rates), but one generated by profit motive is not so acceptable.  Do we want premiums differ based on something that an individual can't control.  So some tough issues.
