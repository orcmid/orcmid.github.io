<a href="http://www.julieleung.com/archives/001370.html">Julie Leung: Seedlings & Sprouts: Emotional Life of Weblogs: BloggerCon III session description</a>.&nbsp; Dave Winer is building the agenda and sessions for the upcoming BloggerCon III down at Stanford at the beginning of November (when we may or may not yet know who is being inaugurated in January, 2005). Naturally, the discussion leaders are publishing their descriptions and inviting interest and comments.&nbsp; I have been commenting on those and not here, because it shows up as more work than I want to do. Silly.

Julie Leung raises great questions about emotions and the way that people reveal themselves on their blogs.&nbsp; I've already left a comment that is longer than her description, and it stays with me still.&nbsp; There's something deep that she's tapped into for me, and I feel like this sleepy old volcano who is being stirred to life by flowing warmth at my core.

I think it is in the wonderful quote from William Golding about being "shut in a bone box and trying to fasten myself onto white paper." Until the end of Julie's description, I didn't get that it is the bone box inside of the skin bag that Golding is speaking of.

And that takes me back to my first-ever trip to Rome, in the country of Vicki's dreams, for the New Years Eve ending of 1997 and our second wedding anniversary.

As we wandered around the ancient areas of Rome, especially the Trajan market and the museum atop Monte Capitolino, I had the experience of walking through a mausoleum.&nbsp; It was as if all of the statuary and edifices were there out of a desparate effort by the Romans to memorialize themselves.&nbsp; No wonder they were captivated by Egypt and things Egyptian, including the struggle with death, I thought to myself. Even the popes who came later manage to leave their mark by providing protective bases (and churches and monuments without end) beneath the Roman and Grecian edifices and statuary that preceded them. It was like "remember me" was being shreiked from every dusty marbled tomb.

On New Years Day, the Appian Way is closed to traffic and people walk it. Vicki and I did that, taking a bus to the outskirts and then walking back on the Appian Way. There, and also later walking in the parks atop Monte Gianicolo I saw how much we have that is a legacy from the Romans and this city. The word for "capitol," brickworks, paving, and civil government. Waterworks and plumbing. Bread and circuses. Graffiti. Supermarkets and malls. We live in the practical inheritance of all of that, made our own by touching and redoing, just like modifying a few lines in someone else's code. This is all the abiding and anonymous legacy of the Romans and others.

I think the drive to be memorialized has not diminished in our seemingly disposable approach to modern society. But I have compassion for it now. And that is how Rome occurs for me when we visit now. 

And blogging
