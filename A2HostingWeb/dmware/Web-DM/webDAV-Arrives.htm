1. Web What?

Don't be confused about XML and WebDAV

WebDAV is a protocol.

  • An extension of HTTP, the protocol that spans the Web

    • Every browser uses it to connect with web sites

    • Every web site uses it to accept requests and create responses

    • It involves accessing sites, requesting information and material, accepting responses, and posting information (e.g., from user-completed forms)

  • The core extensions provide specific support for authoring and managed documents

    • allow entire resources to be created, locked, edited, and replaced

    • provides extensible profile information separate from resources, uses XML as the common carrier of extension information and of document profile information

  • Additional modules of the DAV family provide for

    • versioning, management of collaborative changes, and configuration of complex materials

    • access control on individual resources

    • search across multiple DAV collections

    • extended capability for sharing resources in multiple collections

  • Is oriented around the Web model of URIs as identifications and of resources that are located by URIs [need a little glossary of the toolcraft and lingo around IETF and Web specifications? - sidebar on learning the lingo?]

2. WebDAV's Subtle Intrusion

Subtle Intrusion of WebDAV

The protocol isn't what matters.  It enables something.  We want to move our attention to that.  What it allows is the introduction and substitution/scaling of interoperable fixtures that can be configured, customized, and applied in work practices that involve or require managed documents.

[some place point out how the chicken-and-egg problem was solved for the WebDAV protocol -- the ease of using the Internet as the laboratory and the public availability of clients and servers that can be operated ad lib.  Maybe later.]

The available clients and fixtures that are able to cooperate with document-management services via WebDAV

  • Standard Browsers, including Internet Explorer since 5.x and Netscape ...

  • Web Folders [what are they called in XP?] as a way to bring DAV-reachable document collections to the desktop just like a shared file system

    • the ease of something familiar

    • the ease of migration/scaling from simple to more-organized

    • [look at properties and searching as room for expansion]

  • desktop applications, such as components of Office 2000 and Office XP that manipulate WebDAV-accessed documents as well as any others

  • custom desktop applications for exploring and managing document collections via WebDAV access.

The available services and document-management systems that support organization of collections and their authoring via WebDAV:

  • Web servers, including IIS 5 and Apache with mod_dav installed

  • Public DAV servers that have been placed on the Internet for people to experiment with, perform trials, etc.  [point out this key feature of being able to start easily and to confirm operation -- wonderful for developers to establish compatibility with each other, and also for users to confirm operation]

3. Opportunity or Distraction?

Like it or not, there will be questions about WebDAV.  Why not use it?  Won't it make document management more affordable, doesn't its connection with XML take us into the future?  Can't we extend document management and extraction of organizational knowledge by using WebDAV-supporting products?

We are still in the early adopter stage, so what about that?

4. Scaled Introduction?

Is this an opportunity for exploration, getting ahead of the learningt.  Also, the greatest maturity with DAV is with basics, so it is useful to start with the basics, swimming with water wings, and extending ones reach as opportunities become available for applying extended capabilities of the youngest and in-progress specifications.

Things that DAV doesn't do for you, but that your configuration and integration may need to address:

Establish the profiles that matter to you for your documents.  The basic DAV properties are designed for use in coordinating the protocol, with some elementary ones for the managed resources themselves.  This does not cover application-specific and document-type specific properties that are important in your work settings.  

Discovering the limitations of different services and offerings with regard to how they can model your situation and your profiling, retention, security, auditability, and locatability requirements.  There is more work to be done to increase the available DAV-lore at this level.
